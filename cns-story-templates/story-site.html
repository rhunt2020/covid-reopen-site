The Headline Goes Here

By Emma Grazado & Rachel Hunt


Coronavirus was an “over there” problem – until it wasn’t.


It’s been over a year since the pandemic struck the country collectively. Measures are still being put into and out of place as Maryland navigates the disease’s ripple effects, most recently with Gov. Larry Hogan’s decision to lift all capacity restrictions on indoor and outdoor dining on March 9.


The state has been making decisions based on Gov. Hogan’s “Roadmap to Recovery” plan, which was released on April 24, 2020 and developed based on federal, professional and educational advice. The plan outlines expanding testing and hospital capacities, increasing the supply of personal protective equipment and creating a contact tracing operation as the state’s number one priorities.


The plan is divided into “low, middle and high risk” stages that outline phased rollouts that can happen when the state enters each phase.


“The state has done and continues to do fairly well in terms of compliance with executive orders, and guidance,” Dr. Cliff Mitchell said, who is the director of the environmental health bureau for the Maryland Department of Health.


COVID-19 cases first peaked in May of 2020 as the state entered Phase One of their reopening plan. By August, over 100,000 coronavirus cases had been reported. As the state entered into warmer months, cases and deaths began to temporarily decline. There was a spike in July.


By the time the state was in Phase Three of the plan, cases were beginning to rise higher than they had in the first wave.


Between December and January, Maryland reached an all-time high.


“It breaks my heart that hundreds of thousands of the deaths that we've experienced in this country were preventable and we chose collectively, and our leaders made decisions for us, that put us at risk,” Dr. Neil Seghal, an assistant professor of health policy and management at the University of Maryland, said.



A Timeline of Events

Below is a timeline of closures in Maryland since the state first took action.


March 2020

Primary Closures and Stay at Home Order

March 16, 2020

Gov. Larry Hogan declared the first statewide shutdowns on March 16, 2020, effectively closing all bars, restaurants and movie theaters. Public schools scrambled to make the shift to online and small businesses began to struggle. The state only had around 40 reported cases at that time, but quickly began to take even more intense measures as the numbers rose. By March 30, Gov. Hogan had declared a stay at home order with 1400 cases present and rising, warning that any violations of this order could result in a misdemeanor charge. Less than a month later, a statewide mask mandate was implemented in public areas.


May 2020

Phase 1 Reopening

May 7, 2020

Watch: The Emmett Till Murder

By the time summer was beginning, Gov. Hogan was ready to begin Phase One of Maryland’s reopening plan based on the decline of COVID-19 deaths and hospitalizations. A three-part recovery plan had been made in April, outlining certain activities both outdoor and indoor that could continue once deaths and hospitalizations had declined enough. Phase one meant that activities like outdoor religious services, fishing, boating, golfing, and elective medical procedures could resume, including the reopening of some small businesses. Stipulations for public schools were not clear, but most schools elected to remain online.


June 2020

Phase 2 Reopening

June 5, 2020

Watch: Reporting Little Rock

On June 5, Gov. Hogan lifted the order requiring all non-essential businesses to remain closed. Retail stores, barber shops [not cq] and outdoor dining were included in this. This did not include all bars and restaurants for indoor dining or gyms for use. Businesses like nail salons and tattoo parlors were allowed to reopen at 50% capacity by appointment only. Hogan then left it up to individual counties to make the executive decision on which businesses could reopen.


September 2020

Phase 3 Reopening

Sept. 4, 2020

Watch: Freedom Rides

On Sept. 4, Gov. Hogan moved to Phase 3, allowing all businesses to reopen. Venues like movie theatres could operate at 50% capacity, and retail businesses and places of worship were allowed to open up from 50% capacity to 75%. At the time of the Phase 3 reopening, Hogan said Maryland’s statewide positivity rate had decreased to 3.39%.


November 2020

Half-Capacity Restrictions

Nov. 10, 2020

Watch: Black Press

On Nov. 10, Gov. Hogan said Maryland was in the “danger zone,” as case totals got higher and higher. Commercial food establishments reduced capacity from 75% to 50%, and gatherings were limited to 25 people. State employees were also strongly encouraged to work virtually. Some Maryland counties, like Montgomery County, had already imposed stricter guidelines when Hogan made his announcement.


December 2020

Vaccinations Begin

dec

The University of Maryland Medical Center was the first hospital in the state to receive the Pfizer vaccine, as reported by the Baltimore Sun on Dec. 14. Since then, 51% of Marylanders have received at least one shot of the vaccine. Dr. Seghal said that for many countries other than the U.S., it didn’t take a vaccine to solve the pandemic. “It’s the challenge of different states and different regions having different attitudes towards the pandemic and different attitudes towards mitigation measures,” he said.

"It doesn't take vaccination to try to solve the pandemic."
—Dr. Neil Seghal

January 2021

Thanksgiving and the Holidays

Jan. 1, 2021

As Thanksgiving and the holiday season approached, Maryland health officials strongly advised citizens to celebrate with their immediate households only. In anticipation of homecoming celebrations, Maryland bars and restaurants had a mandated 10 p.m. closing time. Maryland State Police units were also sent out to highly infected areas to monitor for large gatherings. State troopers were also assigned to help enforce COVID-19 guidelines.


March 2021

Capacity Restrictions Lift

March 9, 2021

On March 9, Gov. Hogan announced in a press conference that all capacity restrictions on indoor and outdoor dining would be lifted. At the time of the announcement, about 19% of Marylanders had received at least one dose of the vaccine, according to data from The New York Times.
